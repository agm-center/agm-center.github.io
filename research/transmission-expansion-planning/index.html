Multi-Stage Transmission Expansion Planning Under Uncertainty

Transmission Planning Infrastructure Optimization

Optimizing long-term transmission infrastructure investments considering renewable integration, load growth uncertainty, and emerging technologies like HVDC and FACTS devices.

By AGM Center Research Team

Overview

Transmission expansion planning is critical for accommodating renewable energy, load growth, and maintaining reliability. This research develops robust optimization frameworks for multi-decade planning horizons.

Planning Challenges

Uncertainty Sources

Key uncertainties affecting transmission planning:

  • Load growth: 1-3% annual variation
  • Renewable deployment: Policy-dependent scenarios
  • Technology costs: HVDC, storage, FACTS devices
  • Fuel prices: Natural gas, carbon pricing
  • Extreme weather: Climate change impacts

Decision Complexity

Planning problem characteristics:

  • Time horizon: 30+ years
  • Investment options: 200+ candidate projects
  • Total cost: $50-200B depending on scenario
  • Irreversibility: Most transmission investments permanent

Optimization Methodology

Multi-Stage Stochastic Programming

Mathematical formulation:

  • Stages: 5-year planning periods (6 stages over 30 years)
  • Scenarios: 50 combinations of load, renewable, cost futures
  • Variables: 10,000+ binary (build/don’t build decisions)
  • Constraints: AC power flow, N-1 security, budget limits

Solution Algorithm

Custom decomposition approach:

  1. Benders decomposition: Separate investment and operation decisions
  2. Progressive hedging: Handle scenario tree structure
  3. Parallel computing: Solve 50 scenarios simultaneously
  4. Solution time: 12-24 hours on HPC cluster

Korean Transmission System Analysis

Base Case: 2025-2055

Current system:

  • 765 kV: 1,200 km
  • 345 kV: 12,500 km
  • 154 kV: 32,000 km

Projected needs:

  • 20 GW offshore wind (West/South Sea)
  • 15 GW solar (Southern regions)
  • 5 GW load growth (Seoul/Capital region)

Optimal Expansion Plan

Phase 1: 2025-2030 ($15B)

  • 800 km new 345 kV lines (offshore wind interconnection)
  • HVDC link: West Coast to Capital Region (2 GW)
  • 50 GVar FACTS devices (voltage support)

Phase 2: 2031-2040 ($25B)

  • 1,500 km new 345 kV lines
  • Second HVDC link (3 GW)
  • Grid battery storage (2 GW / 8 GWh)

Phase 3: 2041-2055 ($35B)

  • 765 kV backbone reinforcement
  • HVDC overlay network
  • Advanced grid technologies

FACTS Devices

Strategic deployment of:

  • SVC (Static Var Compensator): Voltage support, 150 units
  • STATCOM: Dynamic reactive support, 80 units
  • UPFC (Unified Power Flow Controller): Congestion relief, 20 units

Benefits:

  • Defer 30% of transmission line investments
  • Improve asset utilization by 25%
  • Enhance stability margins by 40%

Scenario Analysis

Sensitivity to Renewables

Three renewable penetration scenarios:

Low (40% by 2055):

  • Required investment: $55B
  • Mostly AC transmission
  • Traditional planning approach sufficient

Base (60% by 2055):

  • Required investment: $75B
  • Mixed AC/HVDC, significant FACTS
  • Flexibility critical

High (80% by 2055):

  • Required investment: $95B
  • HVDC-dominated, massive storage
  • Radical grid transformation

Flexibility Value

Analysis shows:

  • Deferring irreversible decisions saves $12B (NPV)
  • Modular investments reduce regret by 35%
  • Real options value: $8B

Regulatory Implications

Cost Recovery

Transmission investment recovery mechanisms:

  • Rate-of-return: Traditional utility regulation
  • Performance-based: Incentivize efficiency and innovation
  • Beneficiary pays: Renewable developers share costs
  • Socialized costs: System-wide benefits allocation

Interconnection Policy

Recommendations:

  1. Streamline environmental permitting (reduce from 10 to 5 years)
  2. Proactive planning (build ahead of need)
  3. Cost sharing mechanisms (generation, transmission, distribution)
  4. Public acceptance programs (stakeholder engagement)

Economic Analysis

Cost-Benefit Assessment

Total system costs (2025-2055):

  • Transmission investment: $75B (base scenario)
  • Generation capacity: $180B
  • Energy costs: $450B
  • Total: $705B

Benefits vs. “do nothing”:

  • Renewable integration: $85B (avoided curtailment, carbon value)
  • Reliability: $45B (avoided outages)
  • Congestion relief: $35B (reduced redispatch)
  • Net benefit: $90B

Risk-Adjusted Returns

Monte Carlo analysis (10,000 scenarios):

  • Expected NPV: 30B to $110B)
  • Probability of positive return: 94%
  • Value at Risk (5th percentile): $18B
  • Recommendation: Proceed with base plan, monitor and adapt

International Comparisons

U.S. (Texas ERCOT)

CREZ (Competitive Renewable Energy Zones):

  • $7B investment, 3,600 miles of transmission
  • Enabled 18 GW wind integration
  • Congestion costs reduced by 80%

Europe (North Sea Wind Power Hub)

Multi-country HVDC network:

  • €150B investment through 2050
  • 180 GW offshore wind capacity
  • Cross-border energy trading

China (UHV Network)

Ultra-high voltage (1000 kV AC, ±800 kV DC):

  • World’s longest transmission lines (>3,000 km)
  • Bulk power transfer from West to East
  • $250B invested since 2010

Implementation Roadmap

Short-term (2025-2027)

  • Finalize environmental approvals for Phase 1 projects
  • Issue RFPs for HVDC converters and FACTS devices
  • Pilot advanced technologies (dynamic line rating, topology optimization)

Medium-term (2028-2035)

  • Construct Phase 1 projects
  • Monitor renewable deployment and load growth
  • Update plans based on actual vs. projected outcomes

Long-term (2036-2055)

  • Execute Phases 2 and 3 with adaptive decision-making
  • Integrate emerging technologies (solid-state transformers, superconductors)
  • Continuous stakeholder engagement and plan refinement

Conclusion

Optimal transmission expansion requires balancing long-term vision with near-term flexibility. Our research provides robust, adaptable plans that accommodate uncertainty while ensuring reliable, efficient power delivery for Korea’s energy future.
