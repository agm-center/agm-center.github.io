Causality-Based Network Cost Allocation for Peer-to-Peer Energy Trading

Peer-to-Peer Energy Trading Distribution Network Causality Network Cost Allocation Market Design

A mathematically rigorous framework for assigning network costs based on the causal impact of each peer's trade on voltages, flows, and losses—leading to more efficient and grid-aware P2P energy trading.

By AGM Center Research Team

Overview

Peer-to-peer (P2P) energy trading is rapidly emerging as a flexible and decentralized mechanism for sharing local energy resources.
However, the peer-matching process often ignores physical grid conditions, which leads to:

  • Voltage violations
  • Line congestion
  • Increased system losses

These impacts are ultimately borne by the Distribution System Operator (DSO), limiting the scalability of P2P markets.

This research introduces a causality-based network cost allocation framework that charges peers proportional to the actual physical impact their trades impose on the distribution grid.
Using analytically derived partial derivatives of the power flow equations, the method quantifies how each peer’s trade affects:

  • Voltage magnitudes
  • Line loadings
  • System losses

The result is a grid-aware P2P platform where peers naturally adjust their trading behaviors toward socially optimal outcomes.

Motivation

1. P2P trading often harms the distribution grid

When peer matching ignores physical constraints, it can lead to:

  • Excessive voltage drops
  • Line overloads
  • Higher system losses

These issues reduce network reliability and require DSO intervention.

2. Existing cost-allocation methods are insufficient

MethodWeakness
Universal cost allocationSocializes cost equally → ignores who actually caused the violation
Electrical distance / zonal methodsImprove locality but still do not capture true causality
Constraint-restricting approachesEnsures security, but restricts trades → lowers social welfare
DLMP-based chargingReflects network sensitivity but still dilutes responsibility

None of these methods satisfy both economic fairness and physical accuracy.

3. Need for a market mechanism aligned with physical causality

A principled cost allocation should answer:

Which peer caused how much violation?
And how should they be charged accordingly?

This paper provides the first mathematically rigorous solution.

Methodology

Fig. 1 Overall framework

1. Network Model

The study considers a radial distribution network (\mathcal{D}(\mathcal{N},\mathcal{L})), following DistFlow relationships for:

  • Complex bus voltages (v_n)
  • Line flows (s_l)
  • System losses (o)

2. Peer Modeling

Two peer classes:

  • Sellers: convex quadratic cost
  • Buyers: piecewise quadratic utility

These convex/concave structures guarantee the existence of Nash equilibrium in the P2P market.

3. Defining Network Costs

The network costs include:

  1. Voltage violation cost
  2. Line congestion cost
  3. System loss cost

Each cost is imposed only when exceeding limits.

Causality-Based Cost Allocation

The key innovation is Proposition 1, which derives analytical expressions for the marginal impact of each peer’s trade on grid variables:

[ \frac{\partial |v_n|}{\partial p_k}, \qquad \frac{\partial |s_l|}{\partial p_k}, \qquad \frac{\partial o}{\partial p_k} ]

Using Taylor expansion of the power flow equations, the network cost caused by peer (k) can be approximated as:

[ c_v | \hat{v}_n | \approx c_v \frac{1}{|v_n|} \text{Re}\left( v_n \frac{\partial v_n}{\partial p_k} \right) p_k, ]

Thus, each peer receives a cost proportional to how much their action perturbs the system at the operating point.

This method satisfies:

  • Causality (cost follows responsibility)
  • Fairness (no socialization)
  • Economic optimality

Theoretical Results

1. Universal cost allocation is suboptimal

Proposition 2 proves:

  • Social welfare under universal cost allocation is always lower than the optimal welfare
  • Equality occurs only in the unrealistic case where
    ”all buyers are at one node and all sellers at another”

This highlights that universal allocation cannot achieve market efficiency.

2. Causality-based allocation achieves the social optimum

Proposition 3 shows:

[ W^{c}(P_c^*) = W^{\text{opt}}(P_o^*) ]

Meaning:

  • The Nash equilibrium under causality-based charging
    exactly matches the global welfare optimum
  • Peers naturally adjust to network-friendly trades
  • No need for hard constraints or external penalties

This is the strongest theoretical justification among P2P pricing studies.

Coordination Framework

Fig. 1 (page 4) illustrates a negotiation-based trading mechanism:

  1. DSO computes initial network costs
  2. Peers submit trading decisions
  3. DSO computes causality-based charges:
    • Voltage cost (VC_k)
    • Flow cost (FC_k)
    • Loss cost (LC_k)
  4. Peers update their trading decisions (dual gradient updates)
  5. Process repeats until convergence

This design ensures both:

  • Market-driven trading efficiency
  • Grid-secure operation

Case Study

A modified IEEE 33-node test system is used.

  • 7 selling peers (0.7 MW max each)
  • 17 buying peers (0.38 MW max each)

Two scenarios examined:

Scenario 1 – System loss cost only

Key outcomes:

PolicySocial WelfareSystem Loss
Base581.250.39
Universal580.600.36
Causality-based592.590.28

Notable findings:

  • Universal policy slightly reduces volume for all peers
  • Causality-based policy increases trading for low-impact peers
  • System loss reduced by 22.85%
  • Welfare increased by +11.99 over universal policy

Scenario 2 – All network costs (voltage, congestion, loss)

Fig. 2 (a) Distribution line loading(in % relative to flow limit) and (b) Nodal voltage magnitudes under the base case, universal and causality-based policy.

Under base case:

  • Voltage violations at nodes 7–17
  • Congestion on lines 1–11 and 17–19 (Fig. 2)

Observations:

  • Universal policy reduces volume for every peer
  • Causality-based policy selectively discourages harmful peers
    (e.g., peers causing line overload near nodes 16–17)
  • Congestion is mitigated via peer-specific high costs
    (peer 17 charged $35.04/MWh congestion cost)
  • Low-impact peers can increase trading

This proves that causality-based allocation simultaneously ensures:

  • Voltage and congestion security
  • Higher welfare than universal allocation
  • More efficient use of network limits

Impact

✔ Physically accurate and fair cost allocation

Charges reflect actual causal contribution to violations.

✔ Achieves globally optimal P2P trading outcome

Backed by mathematical proofs.

✔ Enhances grid utilization

Network operates closer to limits without violating constraints.

✔ Reduces system losses

By steering trades toward low-loss paths.

✔ Market-friendly and implementable

Based on dual gradient negotiation framework compatible with existing P2P platforms.

Future Work

  • Extending causality model to multi-period trading
  • Applying to AC power flow and unbalanced networks
  • Incorporating stochasticity in peer preferences
  • Designing incentive-compatible mechanisms that prevent gaming
  • Integration with distribution-level DLMPs and local flexibility markets
