Grid-Scale Energy Storage: Optimal Sizing, Placement, and Operation

Energy Storage Battery Systems Optimization

Comprehensive optimization framework for battery energy storage systems to maximize value across multiple services including energy arbitrage, capacity, and ancillary services.

By AGM Center Research Team

Introduction

Battery energy storage systems (BESS) are becoming economically viable for grid applications. This research optimizes storage deployment to maximize value streams while supporting renewable integration and grid reliability.

Multi-Service Value Optimization

Revenue Streams

Energy Arbitrage:

  • Buy low, sell high based on price differences
  • Value: ₩50-150M per MW-year depending on market volatility

Capacity Services:

  • Provide firm capacity during peak periods
  • Value: ₩200M per MW-year

Frequency Regulation:

  • Fast-response balancing
  • Value: ₩180M per MW-year (highest $/MW service)

Voltage Support:

  • Reactive power provision
  • Value: ₩30M per MW-year

Black Start:

  • Grid restoration capability
  • Value: ₩50M per MW-year

Co-Optimization Framework

Mathematical model maximizes net present value:

Key innovation: Dynamic participation across multiple markets simultaneously while respecting physical constraints.

Optimal Sizing Results

System-Level Analysis

For Korean power system with 40% renewables:

Optimal capacity: 8 GW / 32 GWh (4-hour duration)

Geographic distribution:

  • 40% at renewable generation sites (output smoothing)
  • 35% at load centers (transmission deferral, local services)
  • 25% strategic transmission nodes (congestion relief)

Technology mix:

  • 70% lithium-ion (high power applications)
  • 20% flow batteries (long-duration applications)
  • 10% emerging technologies (solid-state, metal-air)

Site-Specific Sizing

Case study: Seoul metropolitan area

Location: Substation serving 500 MW peak load Optimal size: 50 MW / 100 MWh Value breakdown:

  • Energy arbitrage: 30%
  • Capacity: 25%
  • Frequency regulation: 35%
  • Transmission deferral: 10%

Economics:

  • Total investment: ₩150B
  • Annual revenue: ₩25B
  • Payback: 6 years
  • 20-year NPV: ₩280B

Advanced Control Strategies

Real-Time Operation

Model predictive control for optimal dispatch:

Inputs:

  • Renewable forecasts (solar, wind)
  • Load forecasts
  • Price forecasts (day-ahead, real-time)
  • State of charge
  • Cycle count

Outputs:

  • Charge/discharge schedule
  • Market bids (energy, ancillary services)
  • Reserve withholding

Performance:

  • 18% revenue increase vs. rule-based control
  • Extends battery life by 3-4 years
  • Computation time: <30 seconds (feasible for real-time)

Degradation Modeling

Cycle Life Analysis

Battery degradation factors:

  • Depth of discharge (DoD)
  • C-rate (charge/discharge speed)
  • Temperature
  • Calendar aging

Our model: Physics-based degradation integrated into optimization

Result: Optimal operation balances immediate revenue vs. long-term capacity retention, extending economic life by 25%.

Case Studies

Jeju Island Demonstration

Project: 100 MW / 400 MWh BESS Purpose: Wind integration, frequency stabilization Results:

  • Reduced wind curtailment from 18% to 3%
  • Maintained frequency within ±0.3 Hz during 20+ disturbances
  • ₩18B annual savings (avoided curtailment + reduced reserves)

Industrial Microgrid

Facility: Semiconductor fab with 80 MW peak load Storage: 20 MW / 40 MWh Applications:

  • Peak shaving (reduce demand charges)
  • Power quality (ride through momentary sags)
  • Emergency backup (critical process continuity)

Savings: ₩8B/year, 2.5-year payback

Market Design Recommendations

Product Definitions

Fast frequency response (FFR):

  • Response time: <1 second
  • Duration: 1-5 minutes
  • Payment: $/MW-hour of availability

Regulation:

  • Response time: <10 seconds
  • Duration: 15-30 minutes
  • Payment: $/MW-hour + energy

Load following:

  • Response time: <10 minutes
  • Duration: 1-4 hours
  • Payment: $/MW-hour + energy

Pricing Mechanisms

Capacity mechanism:

  • Annual auction for firm capacity
  • Performance requirements: 95% availability during scarcity events
  • Penalties for non-performance

Real-time co-optimization:

  • Simultaneous clearing of energy and ancillary services
  • Opportunity cost-based pricing
  • 5-minute settlements

Technology Roadmap

Current (2025)

Lithium-ion dominance:

  • Cost: $300-400/kWh (system level)
  • Cycle life: 5,000-8,000 cycles
  • Efficiency: 85-90% round-trip

Near-term (2027-2030)

Performance improvements:

  • Cost: $150-250/kWh (50% reduction)
  • Cycle life: 10,000-15,000 cycles
  • Efficiency: 90-95%

Technology diversification:

  • Sodium-ion for stationary applications (lower cost)
  • Solid-state batteries (higher energy density, safety)
  • Flow batteries for long-duration (8-12 hours)

Long-term (2031-2040)

Transformational technologies:

  • Metal-air batteries (10× energy density)
  • Liquid air energy storage (LAES) for bulk storage
  • Hydrogen storage (seasonal, inter-regional)

System-level innovation:

  • Virtual power plants (aggregated distributed storage)
  • Vehicle-to-grid (millions of EV batteries)
  • Grid-forming capabilities (synthetic inertia, black start)

Economic Analysis

Levelized Cost of Storage (LCOS)

For 4-hour lithium-ion system:

Capital costs:

  • Battery system: $350/kWh
  • Power conversion: $200/kW
  • BOP and installation: 25% markup

O&M costs: $10/kW-year

Lifetime: 15 years, 5,000 cycles

LCOS: $0.12/kWh (cycling once per day)

Comparison:

  • Peaker plants: $0.15-0.25/kWh
  • Pumped hydro: $0.05-0.10/kWh (where available)

Investment Attractiveness

Risk-adjusted returns for grid-scale storage:

Base case (current market rules):

  • IRR: 8-12%
  • Risk level: Moderate (market price volatility)

With supportive policies (capacity payments, carbon pricing):

  • IRR: 14-18%
  • Risk level: Moderate-low

Recommendation: Attractive for private investment with moderate policy support, especially as costs decline.

Policy Recommendations

  1. Technology-neutral markets: Allow storage to compete fairly with generation
  2. Multi-service participation: Enable simultaneous revenue stacking
  3. Interconnection streamlining: Reduce approval timeline from 18 to 6 months
  4. Investment tax credits: 30% ITC for grid-scale storage
  5. Fire safety standards: Develop Korean standards for Li-ion safety

International Lessons

California (1.7 GW deployed):

  • Self-generation incentive program (SGIP): $0.20-0.35/Wh rebate
  • Procurement mandate: 1.3 GW required by utilities
  • Result: Rapid deployment, avoided grid emergencies

Australia (South Australia battery):

  • 150 MW / 194 MWh Hornsdale Power Reserve
  • Frequency regulation: Response time reduced from minutes to milliseconds
  • Savings: $40M/year in ancillary services costs

UK (1 GW pipeline):

  • Enhanced frequency response market
  • Record-high prices for fast services: £50,000/MW-year
  • Business case driven by market design

Future Research Directions

  1. Optimal technology portfolios: Mix of Li-ion, flow, hydrogen, thermal
  2. Degradation-aware control: Advanced aging models integrated into operation
  3. Grid-forming storage: Black start, voltage/frequency control without grid reference
  4. Circular economy: Second-life EV batteries for grid applications
  5. Safety and standards: Fire suppression, thermal management, failure modes

Conclusion

Energy storage is transitioning from niche technology to mainstream grid asset. With costs declining and performance improving, storage can provide enormous value—replacing peaker plants, enabling renewables, and enhancing grid reliability. Our research provides actionable strategies for realizing this potential in Korea and globally.
