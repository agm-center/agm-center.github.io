Advanced Demand Response: Optimization and Market Integration

Demand Response Energy Efficiency Market Design

Leveraging flexible loads and smart buildings to provide grid services, reduce peak demand, and integrate variable renewable energy through advanced DR programs.

By AGM Center Research Team

Executive Summary

Demand response (DR) is evolving from simple load shedding to sophisticated, automated grid resource. This research quantifies DR potential, develops optimal control strategies, and designs market mechanisms for widespread deployment.

DR Resource Assessment

Residential Sector

Flexible load categories in Korean households:

HVAC Systems:

  • Potential: 8 GW (summer peak)
  • Duration: 2-4 hours
  • Customer acceptance: High (minimal comfort impact)
  • Technology: Smart thermostats, AI-based control

Water Heaters:

  • Potential: 3 GW
  • Duration: 1-6 hours (high thermal storage)
  • Customer acceptance: Very high (imperceptible)
  • Technology: Grid-interactive water heaters

EV Charging:

  • Potential: 5 GW by 2030
  • Duration: Flexible (overnight charging)
  • Customer acceptance: Medium (requires managed charging)
  • Technology: Smart charging stations

Total Residential DR: 16 GW (12% of system peak)

Commercial and Industrial

Large Buildings (>10,000 m²):

  • Potential: 4 GW
  • Best for: Frequency regulation, peak shaving
  • Technologies: Building energy management systems (BEMS)

Industrial Facilities:

  • Potential: 6 GW
  • Best for: Long-duration load reduction
  • Technologies: Process control, thermal storage

Total System DR Potential

By 2030:

  • Fast DR (<10 min): 8 GW (frequency regulation)
  • Moderate DR (10-60 min): 12 GW (economic dispatch)
  • Slow DR (1-4 hours): 26 GW (peak shaving)

Optimization Framework

Hierarchical DR Control

Aggregator Level

Aggregators manage portfolios of flexible loads:

  • Objective: Maximize revenue while respecting customer preferences
  • Constraints: Comfort limits, device capabilities, rebound effects
  • Decision variables: Load reduction by device, time period

Mathematical formulation:

System Operator Level

Grid operator coordinates multiple aggregators:

  • Objective: Minimize system cost or maximize reliability
  • Constraints: Power balance, transmission limits, N-1 security
  • Coordination: Price signals or direct dispatch

Multi-Objective Optimization

Balancing competing goals:

  1. Economic efficiency: Minimize total system costs
  2. Customer comfort: Maintain quality of life
  3. Fairness: Equitable burden sharing across customers
  4. Reliability: Ensure DR availability when needed

Pareto-optimal solutions:

  • Use weighted objectives or constraints
  • Allow customer preference specification
  • Provide compensation for flexibility provision

Advanced Control Strategies

Predictive Control

Model Predictive Control (MPC) for smart buildings:

  • Prediction horizon: 24-48 hours
  • Inputs: Weather forecast, occupancy, price signals
  • Outputs: HVAC setpoints, equipment schedules
  • Update frequency: Every 15 minutes

Benefits:

  • 25% energy cost reduction vs. baseline
  • Maintain comfort within ±1°C
  • Provide 2-4 hours of flexible load shifting

Reinforcement Learning

RL-based DR agents:

  • Algorithm: Deep Q-Network (DQN) or Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)
  • State: Temperature, time, price, weather
  • Action: Increase/decrease/hold HVAC setting
  • Reward: Minimize cost + maximize comfort

Performance:

  • Learns optimal strategies in 2-3 weeks
  • Adapts to building-specific characteristics
  • Outperforms rule-based controllers by 15%

Market Design

DR Product Types

Energy Market Products:

  • Day-ahead: Scheduled load reduction
  • Real-time: Fast-response balancing
  • Ancillary services: Reserves, regulation

Capacity Market Products:

  • Seasonal capacity: Committed availability
  • Emergency DR: Last-resort reliability resource

Pricing Mechanisms

Real-Time Pricing (RTP)

Hourly or sub-hourly prices reflecting system conditions:

  • Benefits: Efficient price signals, automated response
  • Challenges: Customer understanding, bill volatility

Critical Peak Pricing (CPP)

High prices during system stress (10-20 events/year):

  • Trigger: 150% of peak load forecast
  • Duration: 2-4 hours
  • Price: 5-10× normal rate

Time-of-Use (TOU)

Fixed on-peak, mid-peak, off-peak prices:

  • Benefits: Predictability, simple to understand
  • Drawbacks: Less responsive to real-time conditions

Compensation Models

Performance-based payments:

  • Baseline establishment (historical usage)
  • Measured reduction vs. baseline
  • Payment: $/kWh reduced

Capacity payments:

  • Enrolled capacity: $/kW-month
  • Availability requirements: 95%+ when called
  • Performance penalties: 110% of capacity payment if fail

Field Demonstrations

Residential Smart Home Pilot

Scope: 5,000 households in Seoul/Busan Duration: 2 years (2023-2024) Technologies: Smart thermostats, water heater controllers, EV charging

Results:

  • Average peak reduction: 1.8 kW per home (22%)
  • Customer satisfaction: 4.2/5.0
  • Annual savings: ₩180,000 per household
  • Scalability: Achieve 8 GW with 4.5M enrolled homes

Commercial Building Program

Scope: 250 large commercial buildings (>10,000 m²) Technologies: BEMS upgrades, real-time optimization

Results:

  • Average demand reduction: 18% during DR events
  • Zero comfort complaints
  • Payback period: 2.8 years
  • Ancillary service revenue: ₩15M per building per year

Economic Analysis

System-Wide Benefits

Annual value of 26 GW DR deployment:

Generation capacity deferral:

  • Avoided peaker plants: 10 GW × ₩1.2M/kW = ₩12 trillion

Energy cost savings:

  • Reduced peak generation costs: ₩4 trillion/year

Transmission/distribution deferral:

  • Delayed infrastructure upgrades: ₩2 trillion

Ancillary services:

  • Reserves and regulation: ₩1.5 trillion/year

Total annual benefits: ₩19.5 trillion ($15B USD)

Customer Economics

Residential participant:

  • Enrollment incentive: ₩100,000
  • Annual DR payments: ₩180,000
  • Energy savings: ₩120,000
  • Total benefit: ₩300,000/year

Commercial participant:

  • Technology investment: ₩50M (amortized over 10 years)
  • Annual DR revenue: ₩25M
  • Energy savings: ₩15M
  • Net benefit: ₩35M/year (payback in 1.4 years)

Regulatory Framework

Program Design Principles

  1. Opt-in participation: Voluntary enrollment
  2. Technology neutral: Allow diverse DR technologies
  3. Performance-based: Pay for actual delivery, not promises
  4. Customer protection: Privacy, override capability, compensation
  5. Competitive markets: Level playing field for aggregators

Policy Recommendations

Near-term (2025-2027):

  • Pilot programs in 3-5 cities
  • Establish measurement and verification protocols
  • Develop aggregator licensing framework

Medium-term (2028-2032):

  • Scale successful programs nationwide
  • Integrate DR into wholesale markets
  • Mandate smart meter deployment (100% coverage)

Long-term (2033+):

  • Fully transactive grid with automated DR
  • Grid-interactive buildings as standard
  • Peer-to-peer energy trading enabled

Challenges and Barriers

Technical Challenges

  1. Baseline accuracy: Establishing counterfactual consumption
  2. Rebound effects: Post-DR surge in demand
  3. Communication reliability: Ensuring signal delivery
  4. Cybersecurity: Protecting customer devices

Economic Barriers

  1. Split incentives: Renters vs. building owners
  2. Upfront costs: Technology investment requirements
  3. Bill uncertainty: Customer risk aversion
  4. Market design: Incomplete valuation of DR benefits

Social Barriers

  1. Customer acceptance: Perceived loss of control
  2. Privacy concerns: Smart meter data
  3. Digital divide: Technology access inequity
  4. Complexity: Understanding programs and benefits

Mitigation Strategies

For each barrier category:

Technical: Advanced metering, predictive analytics, robust communication protocols

Economic: Upfront incentives, performance guarantees, third-party financing

Social: Education campaigns, opt-in design, strong privacy protections, equitable access programs

Future Research

Emerging Technologies

Smart appliances:

  • Grid-integrated HVAC, refrigerators, washers
  • Plug-and-play DR capability
  • Machine learning for autonomous optimization

Building-to-grid integration (B2G):

  • Buildings as distributed energy resources
  • Providing multiple grid services simultaneously
  • Coordinated with solar PV and battery storage

Transactive energy systems:

  • Peer-to-peer energy trading
  • Blockchain for settlements
  • Fully decentralized coordination

Conclusion

Demand response represents one of the most cost-effective grid resources available. With proper market design, technology deployment, and customer engagement, DR can provide gigawatts of flexible capacity—reducing costs, enhancing reliability, and enabling higher renewable penetration. Our research provides actionable roadmaps for realizing this potential.
