Conductor Health-Aware Dynamic Line Rating and Unit Commitment under Uncertainty

Dynamic Line Rating Unit Commitment Conductor Health Asset Management Power System Optimization

A unified framework that integrates conductor thermal degradation, dynamic line rating (DLR), and stochastic unit commitment to enhance renewable integration while protecting transmission assets.

By AGM Center Research Team

Overview

Dynamic Line Rating (DLR) enables transmission operators to extract more capacity from existing lines by leveraging real-time weather data. However, frequent operation at elevated temperatures—called Elevated Temperature Operation (ETO)—accelerates mechanical degradation of conductors.

This research introduces a Conductor Health-Aware Unit Commitment (CHA-UC) framework that incorporates long-term conductor depreciation into daily operational decisions. By combining:

  • A robust linear approximation of conductor temperature
  • A piecewise depreciation cost derived from tensile strength loss
  • A stochastic unit commitment formulation under DLR and renewable uncertainty

CHA-UC prevents excessive thermal stress, reduces wind curtailment, and improves economic and reliability outcomes.

Motivation

Large-scale renewable integration often overloads existing transmission corridors, especially when wind farms are remote from load centers. DLR can significantly relieve congestion by raising thermal limits under favorable weather conditions.

However:

DLR Risk #1 — Elevated Temperature Operation (ETO)

Operating repeatedly at temperatures near or above 95°C reduces the tensile strength of aluminum conductors (LoTS), shortening asset lifetime.

DLR Risk #2 — Forecast Uncertainty

DLR forecasts rely heavily on wind speed—precisely the same factor affecting wind power output.
This introduces correlated uncertainty between transmission limits and renewable generation.

DLR Risk #3 — Cost Blindness in Classical UC

Existing OPF/UC models treat DLR as “free capacity,” ignoring the long-term economic consequences of ETO.

Methodology

Fig. Overall framework

This paper integrates physical conductor aging models, temperature proxies, stochastic optimization, and scenario-based risk management.

1. Modeling Conductor Temperature

Heat Balance Equation

Fig. Illustration of a linear fit to the current-conductor temperature relationship

The current–temperature relationship is derived from the IEEE steady-state heat balance equation (HBE). Although nonlinear, parametric sweeps show that under realistic conditions this relationship is:

  • Convex
  • Well-approximated by a linear proxy

The linear upper-bound constraints are:

[ \tau_{l,t} \ge A_{l,t} p_{l,t} + B_{l,t}, ] [ \tau_{l,t} \ge -A_{l,t} p_{l,t} + B_{l,t} ]

This proxy overestimates temperature slightly—providing a conservative estimate suitable for optimization.

2. Quantifying Conductor Depreciation (ETO → LoTS → Cost)

ETO degrades the aluminum strands inside ACSR conductors.
The paper adopts Harvey’s empirical Loss of Tensile Strength (LoTS) model and extends it to ACSR lines:

  • LoTS increases exponentially with temperature
  • Depreciation accumulates over time
  • High-temperature annealing reduces sensitivity to additional ETO (nonlinear effect)

The depreciation cost for hour (t) under temperature (\tau) is:

[ C^{Dep}_{l,t}(\tau) = B_l S_l D_l \frac{LoTS(\tau) - LoTS(t-1)}{10} ]

To embed this in UC, the cost function is approximated as a piecewise linear convex function (Fig. 3).

3. Conductor Health-Aware Unit Commitment (CHA-UC)

The CHA-UC objective function extends classical UC by adding:

  • Expected conductor depreciation
  • Expected reserve deployment
  • Expected load shedding

[ \text{Total Cost} = \text{Fuel} + \text{Startup/Shutdown} + \text{Expected Depreciation} + \text{Expected Balancing Costs} ]

The stochastic formulation uses:

  • 20 correlated day-ahead scenarios (DLR + wind forecasts)
  • Sample Average Approximation (SAA) for expected costs
  • Scenario-specific temperature constraints

Case Study

Two case studies are conducted on the synthetic Texas 123-bus backbone system (TX-123BT) using NOAA weather data and real DLR simulations.

Case Study I

(Wind power perfect, only DLR uncertainty)

Finding #1 — CHA-UC reduces total cost

MethodAnnual Cost (M$)
SLR21.74
DLR baseline22.05
CHA-UC21.57

➡ CHA-UC saves 0.8% vs SLR and 2.2% vs naive DLR, despite using higher day-ahead costs.

Finding #2 — Wind curtailment reduction

  • SLR: 77.1 MW/day
  • DLR: 9.1 MW/day
  • CHA-UC: 12.7 MW/day

CHA-UC retains most benefits of DLR while avoiding its risks.

Finding #3 — Conductor lifespan preserved

DLR baseline suffers extreme overheating.
CHA-UC reduces depreciation cost by:

  • 3.3× vs SLR
  • 4.8× vs DLR baseline

Result shows CHA-UC avoids “thermal spikes” especially in summer—where ironically SLR becomes risky because ambient conditions approach SLR assumptions.

Finding #4 — Commitment changes protect critical corridors

Fig. Annual heatmaps of day-ahead commitment and commitment changes on map of Texas

  • Cheap units heavily load north–south corridors under DLR
  • CHA-UC commits additional expensive generators to unload critical lines

Impact

✔ Extends conductor lifespan

By explicitly penalizing high-temperature operation.

✔ Reduces renewable curtailment

DLR benefits are preserved while avoiding thermal damage.

✔ Balances short-term and long-term costs

Operational savings vs. asset depreciation are jointly optimized.

✔ Enables smarter transmission asset management

Grid operators can quantify the true cost of aggressive DLR usage.

✔ Applicable to real systems

Validated on a realistic Texas backbone model with actual weather data.

Future Work

  • Extending to AC-OPF and voltage-constrained DLR
  • Incorporating real-time DLR sensor data
  • Multi-year planning with cumulative conductor degradation
  • Joint optimization of DLR, storage, and demand response
  • Market design for DLR-aware ancillary services
