Inertia-Aware Unit Commitment and Market Remuneration for Decarbonized Power Systems

Inertia Unit Commitment Market Design Marginal Pricing Convex Hull Pricing Low-Inertia Systems

A chance-constrained unit commitment framework that co-optimizes energy, reserves, and inertia under renewable uncertainty, along with market mechanisms—marginal pricing, convex hull pricing, average incremental pricing, and uplift—to properly value synchronous and virtual inertia.

By AGM Center Research Team

Overview

As inverter-based renewable energy rapidly replaces synchronous generators (SGs), modern power systems face declining rotational inertia, leading to:

  • Higher RoCoF (Rate of Change of Frequency)
  • Deeper frequency nadirs after contingencies
  • Reduced time for protection systems to act
  • Greater risk of frequency instability

This paper develops:

1) An Inertia-Aware Chance-Constrained Unit Commitment (IA-CCUC)

A UC model that co-optimizes energy, reserves, and inertia while explicitly modeling:

  • Uncertain PV and wind generation
  • Uncertain synthetic inertia from RES
  • Time-coupled SG constraints (ramping, min up/down)
  • ESS inertia and reserve contributions

2) Four remuneration mechanisms for inertia markets

  • Marginal Pricing (MP)
  • Approximate Convex Hull Pricing (aCHP)
  • Average Incremental Pricing (AIP)
  • Uplift payments

3) Comprehensive validation on two systems

  • IEEE 118-bus high-RES test system
  • Korean KPG 193-bus national-scale system

The results offer practical guidelines for designing future inertia markets in low-inertia grids.

Motivation

1. Renewables decrease synchronous inertia

Global PV capacity grew from 40 GW in 2010 to 1,866 GW in 2024.
Wind from 181 GW to 1,133 GW.
This transformation dramatically reduces traditional inertia.

2. Low inertia jeopardizes stability

Low inertia → fast frequency drop → protection misoperation → risk of cascading blackouts.

3. Grid operators increasingly intervene manually

Examples (Table I):

RegionMethodProblems
ERCOTReliability-Must-Run (RMR)Expensive, out-of-market
CAISOMinimum Online CommitmentHigh uplift, inefficient
NESO / AEMOStability marketsEarly-stage designs

These approaches either distort markets or lack long-term investment signals.

4. Inertia needs a market mechanism, not ad-hoc fixes

A transparent, cost-reflective inertia market requires:

  • Correct modeling of uncertainty
  • Proper pricing signals
  • Revenue adequacy for inertia providers
  • Minimal uplift payments

Methodology

1. Modeling Uncertainty of Renewable Power & Inertia

PV and wind power are modeled as:

[ P_{p,i,t} = \hat{P}{p,i,t} - \omega{p,i,t}, \qquad
\omega_{p,i,t} \sim \mathcal{N}(m_{p,i,t}, \sigma^2_{p,i,t}) ]

Similarly, synthetic inertia from RES is modeled stochastically.

Thus, uncertainty enters both energy balance and inertia adequacy.

2. Chance-Constrained Inertia-Aware UC

The optimization includes:

Synchronous Generator Constraints

  • Min up/down time
  • Ramping limits
  • Reserve participation

Chance constraints for SG output

Ensuring:

[ P^{\min} \le P_{g,i,t} \le P^{\max} ]

with probability ≥ (1 - \epsilon).

Energy Storage Participation

ESS contributes:

  • Fast reserves
  • Synthetic inertia
  • Frequency-nadir safety margins

System-wide inertia adequacy

[ \sum (\text{SG inertia} + \text{ESS inertia} + \text{PV inertia} + \text{WT inertia}) \ge H_{\min}^{sys} ]

Chance constraints ensure adequacy under uncertainty.

3. Deterministic Reformulation

Using Gaussian assumptions, chance constraints become linear by replacing random variables with:

[ \hat{\delta} = \Phi^{-1}(1 - \epsilon) \Sigma - M ]

allowing the full chance-constrained UC to be solved as MIQP.

Remuneration Mechanisms for Inertia

1. Uplift

Covers unrecovered fixed/start-up costs.

Pros: Ensures revenue adequacy
Cons: No price signal for investments; distorts the market

2. Marginal Pricing (MP)

Inertia price = dual variable of inertia constraint.

But…

  • Often zero because inertia constraint is not binding
  • Start-up costs do not enter inertia pricing
  • Requires large uplift payments

3. Approximate Convex Hull Pricing (aCHP)

Key idea:

  • Relax commitment: ( 0 \le u_{g,i,t} \le 1 )
  • Distribute start-up costs over active hours
  • Solve convexified problem each hour

This allows:

  • Start-up & fixed costs to enter inertia price
  • Inertia constraint to become binding → non-zero inertia price

This is the strongest price signal of all methods.

4. Average Incremental Pricing (AIP)

Converts fixed + start-up costs into average incremental cost:

[ \hat{b}{g,i} = b{g,i} + \frac{c^{NL}g + SU_g}{\sum_t P^*{g,i,t}} ]

Pros:

  • Higher energy & reserve prices → better cost recovery
  • Moderate inertia pricing

Cons:

  • Inertia price still undervalues true marginal inertia
  • Some uplift still required

Case Study 1: IEEE 118-bus System

1. Inertia Adequacy

Fig. 1 System intertia levels provided by all generation resources under different scenarios and renewable penetration levels. The dashed line denotes the inertia requirement.

  • Base w/o inertia → fails requirements during off-peak hours
  • IA-UC (MP, aCHP, AIP) → always satisfies inertia
  • Requires fewer SGs online than RMR-based approach

2. Frequency Stability

Loss of 1500 MW generator at 01:00 →
Frequency nadir improves from:

  • 59.062 Hz → 59.139 Hz
  • RoCoF improves ~50%

(Inertia-aware methods outperform base case significantly)

3. Energy, Reserve, and Inertia Prices

Result shows that

  • aCHP energy prices > MP by 8.82%
  • AIP prices > aCHP by ~1.38%
  • Inertia price:
    • MP: nearly zero
    • AIP: moderate
    • aCHP: highest → best signal

4. Unit Profitability

Result shows that

  • Base-load units → profitable in all cases
  • Mid-merit → MP undercompensates, aCHP ensures cost recovery
  • Peakers → MP severely underpays; aCHP provides adequate inertia revenue

Case Study 2: KPG 193 Korean System

1. Inertia adequacy

  • KPG193 requires ~306 GW·s
  • IA-UC solutions maintain adequacy even at high RES levels (15–20%)

2. Frequency response

  • RoCoF improves 35% under inertia-aware methods
  • Frequency nadir significantly improved during low-inertia periods

3. Commitment patterns

  • MP / aCHP / AIP replace expensive RMR units
  • IA-UC commits fewer SGs than RMR approach
  • Cost savings with same inertia level

4. Market outcomes

  • aCHP produces highest inertia prices
  • MP produces nearly zero inertia price → largest uplift
  • AIP sits between the two

5. Policy implications for Korea

  • aCHP reduces dependence on RMR
  • AIP improves transitional price adequacy
  • Explicit inertia prices needed to incentivize peakers and mid-merit SGs

Key Insights

✔ Chance-constrained inertia-aware UC stabilizes frequency

Improves both nadir and RoCoF under uncertainty.

✔ aCHP delivers the most accurate & economically consistent inertia price

Strongest investment signal, lowest uplift.

✔ MP systematically undervalues inertia

Poor price signal → highest uplift needed.

✔ AIP is a middle-ground solution

Partial cost internalization → moderate uplift.

✔ KPG193 validation confirms scalability

Applicable to national-scale low-inertia systems.

Impact

This work provides:

For system operators

  • A framework to incorporate inertia into market clearing
  • Criteria to replace RMR-based reliability approaches
  • Tools to evaluate inertia adequacy under renewables uncertainty

For policymakers

  • Evidence that convex hull–based pricing better supports long-term investment
  • Clear comparison of remuneration mechanisms
  • Practical design guidelines for future inertia markets

For researchers

  • A unified testbed (IEEE118 + KPG193) for low-inertia UC studies
  • Chance-constrained formulations for SG/ESS/RES inertia
  • Pricing models bridging UC, convex analysis, and stochastic markets

Future Work

  • Extending to AC-UC and full frequency security constraints
  • Modeling fast frequency response (FFR) jointly with inertia
  • Incorporating IBR control dynamics more explicitly
  • Developing market simulation tools for inertia & stability services
  • Evaluating multi-day, multi-season inertia requirements

Reference
