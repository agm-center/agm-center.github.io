Causality-Based Cost Allocation for Peer-to-Peer Energy Trading

Peer-to-Peer Trading Distribution Networks Network Cost Allocation Grid-Aware Markets

A clear explanation of how causality-based pricing makes P2P trading fairer and more grid-friendly in distribution systems.

By AGM Center Research Team

ü§ù Making P2P Energy Trading Fair and Grid-Friendly

Figure 1. Peer-to-peer enegy trading.(Image source: Pulse Energy)

Peer-to-peer (P2P) energy trading lets small consumers and generators exchange electricity directly.
It promotes community energy use and renewable adoption ‚Äî but it also interacts with the physical grid.

The problem?

Some trades stress the grid far more than others ‚Äî but current pricing treats everyone the same.

This can cause:

  • voltage violations
  • line congestion
  • increased system losses
  • unfair cost allocation

This paper introduces a causality-based cost allocation method that charges peers based on the actual impact their trades impose on the distribution network.

‚ö° Why Existing Approaches Fall Short

1) Universal cost allocation

Everyone pays the same network fee per MWh.

Issue:
Harmless peers subsidize harmful ones ‚Üí inefficient and unfair.

2) Distance or zone-based fees

Uses electrical distance or zone grouping.

Issue:
Distance ‚â† true physical impact. Not aligned with real-time grid behavior.

3) Hard constraints

Block trades that violate limits.

Issue:
Too restrictive. Prevents beneficial trades and lowers total welfare.

üß† Key Idea: Charge Peers According to Causality

Instead of spreading costs evenly, the proposed method:

  1. Observes each peer‚Äôs trade.
  2. Measures how much that specific trade changes:
    • system losses
    • line flows
    • voltage levels
  3. Charges cost only to the peers who caused those changes.
  4. Peers adjust their trading decisions accordingly.

Result:

Grid-friendly trades are encouraged; harmful trades become more expensive.

No banning trades. No punishing everyone.
Just meaningful, physics-based price signals.

üó∫ Test System: IEEE 33-Bus Distribution Feeder

Figure 2. Test feeder with 7 sellers and 17 buyers spread across the network.

This radial feeder highlights classic distribution-level challenges:

  • weak voltages
  • long feeders
  • congestion near the substation

Perfect for evaluating grid-aware pricing.

Policy Social Welfare System Loss Trading Volume
Base 581.25 0.39 4.67
Universal 580.60 0.36 4.50
Causality-Based 592.59 0.28 4.53

What this means:

  • Universal policy lowers everyone‚Äôs trades ‚Üí welfare barely improves.
  • Causality-based charges reduce loss-causing trades only.
  • Total system loss down 22.8%, welfare up highest of all policies.

Effective and fair.

üìà Scenario 2 ‚Äî Voltage, Congestion, and Loss

Policy Voltage Security Trading Volume Social Welfare
Base Violations 4.67 581.25
Universal Secure but over-conservative 1.76 351.14
Causality-Based Secure & efficient 2.97 498.35

Interpretation:

  • Universal overreacts ‚Üí everyone trades less ‚Üí grid underutilized.
  • Causality-based policy signals who causes violations.
    • High-impact peers reduce trades.
    • Low-impact peers trade more.
    • Grid becomes secure without sacrificing welfare.

üîß Example: Who Actually Pays More?

The method naturally identifies:

  • Peers near congested lines ‚Üí pay higher congestion cost
  • Peers causing voltage drop ‚Üí pay higher voltage cost
  • Peers with minimal impact ‚Üí pay almost nothing

This is true cost causation, not approximation.

üí° Why This Matters

‚úî Fair

Peers pay based on actual impact, not arbitrary fees.

‚úî Efficient

Trading adjusts intelligently rather than uniformly shrinking.

‚úî Improves social welfare

Because good trades are encouraged while harmful ones diminish.

‚úî Maintains grid security

Voltage & congestion remain within limits ‚Äî without banning trades.

‚úî Better than universal cost allocation

Universal is simple but inefficient; causality-based is both principled and practical.
