Hydrogen Energy Systems: Production, Storage, and Grid Integration

Hydrogen Energy Storage Decarbonization

Exploring hydrogen's role in decarbonization through analysis of electrolysis, fuel cells, and hydrogen-fired power generation for seasonal storage and firm capacity.

By AGM Center Research Team

Introduction

Hydrogen offers unique advantages for deep decarbonization: long-duration energy storage, seasonal shifting, and zero-carbon dispatchable generation. This research evaluates hydrogen’s technical and economic viability for Korean power systems.

Hydrogen Production

Electrolysis Technologies

Alkaline electrolyzers:

  • Mature technology, lowest cost
  • Efficiency: 60-70% (HHV basis)
  • Cost: $500-1,000/kW
  • Suitable for large-scale, steady-state operation

Proton exchange membrane (PEM):

  • Fast response, compact
  • Efficiency: 60-70%
  • Cost: $1,000-2,000/kW
  • Suitable for variable renewable coupling

Solid oxide electrolysis cells (SOEC):

  • Highest efficiency (80-90% with waste heat)
  • Cost: $2,000-4,000/kW (early stage)
  • High temperature operation (700-900°C)
  • Future technology, 2030+ commercial readiness

Green Hydrogen Potential

Korean renewable hydrogen production potential:

Offshore wind:

  • 20 GW offshore wind by 2030
  • Capacity factor: 40%
  • Hydrogen production: 1.2M tons/year (50% of generation to electrolysis)

Solar PV:

  • Curtailed solar energy (over-generation periods)
  • ~5 TWh/year by 2035 (5% of solar generation)
  • Hydrogen production: 0.15M tons/year

Total green hydrogen: 1.35M tons/year by 2035

Production Costs

Levelized cost of hydrogen (LCOH):

Today (2025):

  • Electricity: $50/MWh (renewable)
  • Electrolyzer capex: $1,000/kW, 10-year life
  • LCOH: $5.5/kg

Future (2035):

  • Electricity: $30/MWh (cheaper renewables)
  • Electrolyzer capex: $400/kW (scale economies)
  • LCOH: $2.5/kg

Comparison:

  • Gray hydrogen (natural gas): $1.5/kg (but emits CO₂)
  • Blue hydrogen (natural gas + CCS): $2.5/kg
  • Green hydrogen competitive by 2035

Storage and Transportation

Storage Technologies

Compressed gas:

  • Pressure: 350-700 bar
  • Storage density: 20-40 kg/m³
  • Cost: $400-800/kg H₂ capacity
  • Suitable for: Medium-term (days to weeks)

Liquid hydrogen:

  • Temperature: -253°C
  • Storage density: 71 kg/m³
  • Cost: $1,000-1,500/kg H₂ capacity
  • Energy loss: 30% for liquefaction
  • Suitable for: Long-distance transport

Underground storage (salt caverns):

  • Large scale (thousands of tons)
  • Cost: $1-3/kg H₂ capacity
  • Suitable for: Seasonal storage (months)

Metal hydrides:

  • Solid-state storage (safe, compact)
  • Cost: $1,500-3,000/kg H₂ capacity
  • Suitable for: Distributed applications

Transportation Infrastructure

Pipelines:

  • Existing natural gas pipelines: Blend up to 15-20% H₂
  • Dedicated H₂ pipelines: New infrastructure, $1-2M/km
  • Suitable for: Large volumes, short-to-medium distance

Trucks:

  • Compressed or liquid tankers
  • Cost: $2-5/kg H₂ per 500 km
  • Suitable for: Distributed delivery, flexible routing

Shipping:

  • Liquid H₂ or ammonia carriers
  • International imports (Australia, Middle East)
  • Cost: $1-3/kg H₂ per 5,000 km

Power Generation

Hydrogen Fuel Cells

Proton exchange membrane fuel cells (PEMFC):

  • Efficiency: 50-60% (electricity only)
  • Fast start (<1 minute)
  • Scalable: 1 kW to 10 MW+
  • Applications: Distributed generation, backup power

Solid oxide fuel cells (SOFC):

  • Efficiency: 60-70% (electricity), 85%+ (combined heat and power)
  • Slower start (hours)
  • High capital cost
  • Applications: Baseload power, industrial CHP

Hydrogen Turbines

Retrofitted gas turbines:

  • Existing turbines modified for H₂ combustion
  • Fuel flexibility: 0-100% H₂ blending
  • Efficiency: 40-50% (simple cycle), 60%+ (combined cycle)
  • Cost: $50-200M for 500 MW plant upgrade

Purpose-built H₂ turbines:

  • Optimized for pure hydrogen
  • Efficiency: 65%+ (CCGT)
  • Availability: 2028+ for commercial deployment

Economic Comparison

Levelized cost of electricity (LCOE) for H₂ power:

Hydrogen turbine (100 MW, 20% capacity factor):

  • Capex: $1,000/kW
  • H₂ fuel: $3/kg, efficiency 50%
  • LCOE: $300/MWh

Comparison:

  • Natural gas peaker: $150/MWh
  • Battery storage (4-hour): $200/MWh
  • Advantage for H₂: Long-duration (>24 hours), seasonal storage

Grid Integration

Operational Roles

Seasonal storage:

  • Store excess summer solar as H₂
  • Generate electricity in winter (high heating demand)
  • Capacity: Store 100 GWh (equivalent to 3M kg H₂)

Long-duration firming:

  • Back up multi-day renewable droughts
  • “Dunkelflaute” events (no wind or sun for 5+ days)
  • H₂ turbines provide firm capacity

Industrial decarbonization:

  • Steel production (direct reduced iron)
  • Petrochemicals (ammonia, methanol synthesis)
  • Cement (high-temperature heat)

Optimal Deployment

System modeling results (2035, 60% renewable target):

Without hydrogen:

  • Battery storage: 15 GW / 60 GWh
  • Gas peakers: 20 GW (high emissions or carbon capture required)
  • Renewable curtailment: 12%
  • Total system cost: ₩85T

With hydrogen:

  • Battery storage: 10 GW / 40 GWh (reduced needs)
  • H₂ electrolysis: 5 GW (absorb excess renewables)
  • H₂ turbines: 5 GW (firm capacity)
  • H₂ storage: 500 GWh (seasonal)
  • Renewable curtailment: 3%
  • Total system cost: ₩80T

Savings: ₩5T (6%), plus zero-carbon firm capacity

Economic Analysis

Value Streams

Electrolyzer revenue:

  • Negative price arbitrage (paid to consume excess power)
  • Grid services (provide demand flexibility)
  • Value: $20-50/kW-year

H₂ storage value:

  • Seasonal arbitrage (summer-winter price spreads)
  • Long-duration backup
  • Value: $15/kg H₂ capacity-year

H₂ generation revenue:

  • Energy market (scarcity pricing during renewable droughts)
  • Capacity market (firm capacity value)
  • Value: $100-200/kW-year

Total System Economics

Hydrogen infrastructure investment (2025-2040):

  • Electrolyzers: ₩5T
  • Storage: ₩3T
  • Turbines: ₩4T
  • Pipelines: ₩2T
  • Total: ₩14T

Benefits:

  • Renewable integration: ₩8T (avoided curtailment)
  • Firm capacity: ₩10T (avoided peaker plants)
  • Industrial decarbonization: ₩15T (carbon value at ₩100/ton)
  • Total: ₩33T

Net benefit: ₩19T, IRR 12%

Safety and Environmental

Safety Considerations

Hydrogen properties:

  • Highly flammable (wider flammability range than natural gas)
  • Buoyant (disperses quickly, reducing pool fire risk)
  • Odorless, colorless (requires leak detection systems)

Mitigation measures:

  • Comprehensive leak detection (continuous monitoring)
  • Ventilation systems (prevent accumulation)
  • Safety standards (IEC 60079, ISO 14687)
  • Training and protocols (emergency response)

Historical safety record: Comparable to natural gas when properly handled

Life-Cycle Emissions

Green hydrogen (renewable electrolysis):

  • Production: 0-5 kg CO₂e/kg H₂ (embodied emissions in electrolyzer, minimal)
  • Transport/storage: <1 kg CO₂e/kg H₂
  • Combustion: 0 kg CO₂ (only water vapor)
  • Total: <6 kg CO₂e/kg H₂

Comparison:

  • Gray hydrogen: 10-12 kg CO₂/kg H₂
  • Blue hydrogen: 2-4 kg CO₂/kg H₂
  • Natural gas power: 400-500 kg CO₂/MWh
  • Green H₂ power: <20 kg CO₂/MWh

Policy Framework

Government Support

Production incentives:

  • Capital subsidies for electrolyzers (30-50%)
  • Operating subsidies (bridge LCOH gap to $3/kg)
  • Tax credits (similar to investment tax credit for renewables)

Infrastructure development:

  • Public investment in pipelines and storage
  • H₂ refueling stations (transportation sector)
  • R&D funding ($500M/year)

Market design:

  • Hydrogen trading platform (pricing transparency)
  • Guarantee of origin (certify green hydrogen)
  • Procurement mandates (government and utilities)

International Cooperation

Import strategy:

  • Partnerships with Australia, Middle East (cheap renewable resources)
  • Ammonia as H₂ carrier (easier transport)
  • Target: 50% domestic, 50% imported by 2050

Technology collaboration:

  • Joint R&D with Japan, EU, USA
  • Standardization (safety, quality, compatibility)
  • Knowledge sharing (accelerate learning curve)

Implementation Roadmap

Phase 1: Demonstration (2025-2028)

  • 500 MW electrolysis capacity
  • 5 GWh underground H₂ storage
  • 2 hydrogen turbine demonstrations (50 MW each)
  • Industrial pilot projects (steel, chemicals)

Phase 2: Scale-up (2029-2035)

  • 5 GW electrolysis capacity
  • 100 GWh storage
  • 2 GW H₂ turbines
  • Hydrogen pipeline network (500 km)

Phase 3: Mainstream (2036-2050)

  • 15+ GW electrolysis
  • 500 GWh seasonal storage
  • 10+ GW firm H₂ generation
  • Fully integrated energy system (power, industry, transport)

Challenges and Outlook

Key challenges:

  1. Cost competitiveness (need $2-3/kg green H₂)
  2. Infrastructure build-out (pipelines, storage)
  3. Efficiency losses (round-trip ~30-40%)
  4. Supply chain (electrolyzers, turbines)
  5. Regulatory framework (safety, market design)

Outlook:

  • Technology rapidly maturing (costs falling 20%/year)
  • Strong policy support (Korea, EU, USA, Australia)
  • Growing demand (industry, transportation, power)
  • Hydrogen essential for deep decarbonization (80%+ emissions reduction)

Conclusion

Hydrogen is not a silver bullet, but it fills critical gaps in the zero-carbon energy system—especially long-duration storage and firm capacity. With declining costs and strong policy support, hydrogen can become economically viable and play a major role in Korea’s decarbonization by 2050. Our research provides strategies for optimal hydrogen integration into power systems.
