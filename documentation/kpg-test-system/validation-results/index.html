The validation ensures:

  1. Power flow feasibility: AC power flow converges for realistic operating points

Power Flow Validation

Objective: Verify network can support typical operating conditions

Method:

  1. Load MATPOWER case with base demand levels
  2. Set generator outputs to reasonable dispatch
  3. Solve AC power flow using Newton-Raphson
  4. Check convergence and constraint violations

Solver: Ipopt (Interior Point Optimizer)

Results:

Example — AC power flow verification:

Voltage profile characteristics:

  • 765 kV buses: 1.03-1.05 p.u. (tight regulation)
  • 345 kV buses: 1.00-1.05 p.u. (normal operating range)
  • 154 kV buses: 0.98-1.08 p.u. (wider tolerance)

Unit Commitment Validation

Objective: Verify sufficient generation capacity exists to meet demand throughout the year with 365-day UC study

Method:

  1. Solve day-ahead UC for each of 365 days
  2. Use tight MILP formulation (Morales-España et al., 2013)
  3. Include all constraints: min up/down time, ramps, reserves
  4. Check feasibility and compute system costs

Formulation details:

  • Objective: Minimize generation + startup + shutdown costs
  • Constraints: Power balance, generator limits, ramping, min up/down times, reserves
  • Special: Nuclear must-off periods enforced
  • Renewable: Curtailment allowed (not must-take)

Solver: HiGHS or Gurobi with 0.5% MIP gap, 3600s time limit

Results summary:

Annual statistics:

  • Peak demand day: Day 200 (mid-July summer peak)
  • Peak system demand: ~83,000 MW
  • Online capacity at peak: ~95,000 MW
  • Reserve margin: ~15% (adequate for reliability)
  • Annual average commitment: ~85 generators online

Capacity adequacy:

The system maintains adequate generation capacity throughout the year:

Typical commitment levels:

  • Night (00:00-06:00): 70-80 generators (baseload + must-run)
  • Day (10:00-18:00): 90-100 generators (full load following)
  • Peak (18:00-21:00): 95-105 generators (all available units)

Technology-specific patterns:

Nuclear (22 units):

  • Run continuously at high output (95% Pmin, near inflexible)
  • Only offline during scheduled must-off periods
  • Provide ~25-30 GW of baseload

Coal (46 units):

  • Provide baseload and mid-merit generation
  • Typical commitment: 35-40 units online
  • Longer minimum up/down times

LNG (54 units):

  • Flexible load following
  • Commitment varies: 25-40 units based on demand
  • Faster ramping capability

AC Optimal Power Flow Validation

Objective: Verify system can be optimally dispatched for all 8760 hours while respecting AC power flow physics

Method:

  1. Fix commitment from UC solution
  2. Solve AC-OPF for each hour independently
  3. Minimize generation cost subject to AC power flow equations
  4. Enforce voltage limits, thermal limits, generator limits

Formulation:

  • Variables: Active power (P), reactive power (Q), voltage magnitude (V), voltage angle ()
  • Objective: Minimize generation cost
  • Constraints: AC power balance, Kirchhoff’s laws, V/I limits

Solver: Ipopt with default tolerances

Results summary:

Convergence characteristics:

  • Iterations to convergence: Typically 10-30 iterations
  • Objective values: Consistent with linear approximation (±1-3%)
  • Voltage magnitudes: Within ±5% of nominal
  • Real power losses: 3-5% of total generation (realistic)

Power flow pattern validation:

The AC-OPF results exhibit realistic power flow patterns matching known Korean grid characteristics:

1. Long-Distance Power Transfer

Major power flows from coastal generation to capital region:

  • From southeastern coast (nuclear/coal) → To Seoul area
  • Distance: 200-300 km transmission paths
  • Voltage level: Primarily 765 kV and 345 kV backbone
  • Flow magnitude: Up to 15,000 MW during peak hours

2. Jeju Island HVDC Operation

  • HVDC link utilization: 30-80% of capacity depending on hour
  • Direction: Mostly mainland → Jeju (import) during peaks
  • Local generation: Jeju uses local renewables when available
  • Realistic operation: Matches actual HVDC operation patterns

Hardware: Standard research workstation (16 cores, 64 GB RAM)

Scalability: Suitable for:

  • Detailed operational studies (ED, UC)
  • Planning studies (capacity expansion)
  • Large-scale scenario analysis (hundreds of cases)
  • Uncertainty quantification (Monte Carlo)

Solver performance:

UC solving (HiGHS):

  • Variables: ~8,000 per day (122 units × 24 hours × 3 vars)
  • Constraints: ~25,000 per day
  • Binary variables: ~3,000
  • Solution time: 1-5 minutes/day (0.5% gap)
  • Parallel: Can solve multiple days independently

AC-OPF solving (Ipopt):

  • Variables: ~1,500 per hour (buses + branches + generators)
  • Constraints: ~2,000 per hour (power balance + limits)
  • Nonlinear: AC power flow equations
  • Solution time: 2-5 seconds/hour
  • Robust: Converges from flat start

Snapshot ACOPF Analysis

A detailed snapshot AC-OPF study was performed to validate specific grid characteristics.

Representative Hour Study

Selected hour: Peak demand hour on summer day (Day 200, Hour 18)

Analysis:

  1. Solve AC-OPF for peak hour
  2. Examine power flow patterns
  3. Validate voltage profiles
  4. Check generation dispatch
  5. Verify transmission utilization

Power flow results:

Validation: These patterns match reported power flows in actual Korean grid during summer peaks.

Voltage results:

Voltage magnitude distribution:

  • 765 kV buses: 1.035 ± 0.015 p.u. (tight regulation)
  • 345 kV buses: 1.010 ± 0.035 p.u. (normal operation)
  • 154 kV buses: 0.990 ± 0.055 p.u. (wider variation)

Observation: Voltage drops along transmission paths from generation to load, with lowest voltages in heavily loaded Seoul area buses (0.96-1.00 p.u.). This realistic pattern confirms AC power flow equations are properly enforced.

Transmission utilization:

  • 765 kV backbone: 40-70% of thermal rating (well within limits)
  • 345 kV interregional: 50-85% of rating (moderate utilization)
  • 154 kV regional: 30-60% of rating (light to moderate)
  • Critical corridors: Seoul approach paths at 75-85%

No overloads observed in base case, confirming transmission adequacy.

Known Limitations

The validation also identified limitations that users should be aware of:

1. Reactive power compensation

No reactive compensation devices (SVCs, STATCOMs, tap changers) modeled.

Impact:

  • Voltage control less flexible than actual grid
  • May not capture voltage stability issues accurately
  • AC-OPF may show different reactive dispatch than reality

Mitigation: Users can add compensation devices for specific studies

2. Transmission detail

Some transmission lines aggregated or simplified.

Impact:

  • Local congestion patterns not fully captured
  • Inter-area transfer limits approximate
  • N-1 contingency analysis may differ from actual grid

Mitigation: Focus on system-level studies rather than specific line analysis

3. Reactive load modeling

Reactive demand fixed at Q = 0.1 × P.

Impact:

  • Load power factor assumed constant
  • Voltage-dependent loads not modeled
  • Dynamic reactive compensation by loads ignored

Mitigation: Reasonable approximation for planning studies

4. OSM data uncertainty

Network topology from OpenStreetMap has update lag.

Impact:

  • Some recent line additions may be missing
  • Decommissioned lines may still be present
  • Line parameters are estimates

Mitigation: Validated against known major transmission paths

5. Single weather year

Only 2022 weather data available.

Impact:

  • Cannot study multi-year renewable variability
  • Extreme weather events specific to 2022
  • Drought/flood impacts on hydro not generalizable

Mitigation: Use as typical year; can substitute other weather years
