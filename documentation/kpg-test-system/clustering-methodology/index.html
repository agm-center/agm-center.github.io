Clustering Methodology

Clustering Methodology

The KPG 193 is a synthetic power grid test system developed using open-source data and spatial clustering techniques. This page explains the methodology behind its construction, as detailed in Song & Kim (2024).

Challenges with actual grid data in Korea:

  1. National security: Detailed power grid information classified as critical infrastructure
  2. Commercial sensitivity: Generator costs, fuel contracts, market strategies are proprietary
  3. Data unavailability: Network topology not publicly disclosed
  4. Legal restrictions: Cannot freely share or publish sensitive grid information
  5. Dynamic changes: Real grid evolves continuously, making reproducibility difficult

Benefits of synthetic data:

  1. Open access: Free to use, modify, and share for research
  2. Reproducible: Consistent dataset for comparing research results
  3. Well-documented: All parameters and methods publicly described
  4. Legally shareable: No confidentiality or security restrictions
  5. Scenario flexibility: Easy to modify for future grid studies

Network topology (OpenStreetMap):

Extracted elements:

  • Power lines: Overhead transmission lines (power=line)
  • Power cables: Underground transmission cables (power=cable)
  • Substations: High-voltage substations (power=substation)
  • Voltage levels: 765 kV, 345 kV, 154 kV classifications
  • Line parameters: Voltage ratings, number of circuits

Extraction method:

  • Overpass API queries for Korean power infrastructure
  • Filtering by voltage level (≥154 kV transmission)
  • Topology validation and correction

Limitations of OSM data:

  • Update schedule uncertain (may lag real grid changes)
  • Some topology errors or missing connections
  • Line parameters estimated from standard values

Line parameter calculation:

Standard conductor coefficients from manufacturer catalogs used to compute:

  • Resistance (R): Based on conductor type and voltage level
  • Reactance (X): Calculated from line geometry
  • Susceptance (B): Computed from capacitance
  • Thermal ratings: Typical values for voltage class

Reference: Grigsby (2007), Electric Power Generation, Transmission, and Distribution

Conventional generators:

Available information:

  • Total installed capacity by technology (Coal, LNG, Nuclear)
  • Approximate unit sizes and counts
  • Regional distribution (province level)
  • Plant names and general locations

Renewable generators:

Municipal-level data (230 segments):

  • Solar PV installed capacity by municipality
  • Wind farm capacity by municipality
  • Small hydro capacity by municipality
  • Installation counts and size distribution

Dataset: KEA supply statistics (2022), cumulative installed capacity by region

Weather data:

LDAPS (Local Data Assimilation and Prediction System):

  • Operational weather forecasting model
  • Spatial resolution: ~5 km grid
  • Temporal resolution: Hourly
  • Coverage: Full year 2022

Variables extracted:

  • Temperature: 2-meter air temperature (K)
  • Wind speed: Wind velocity at 93m hub height (m/s)
  • Wind components: U (east-west) and V (north-south) vectors
  • Radiation: Net downward longwave flux (W/m²)

These variables used to generate renewable generation profiles.

Electrical load:

Available data:

  • Municipal-level hourly electricity consumption (230 municipalities)
  • Full year 2022 (8,760 hours)
  • Disaggregated by city/county/district

Dataset: KPX hourly electricity sales volume by region (2022)

Characteristics:

  • Strong summer peak (air conditioning)
  • Concentrated in Seoul metropolitan area (~40% of load)
  • Industrial demand base in southeastern regions

Clustering Approach

The core innovation of KPG 193 is the spatial clustering strategy that reduces complexity while preserving system characteristics.

Problem:

  • Demand and renewable data available for 230 municipalities
  • Creating 230-bus system would be computationally expensive
  • Need to aggregate while maintaining:
    • Geographic distribution
    • Load patterns
    • Generation locations
    • Transmission structure

Solution: Cluster based on utility company regional structure

Clustering Method

Key insight: Use Korea Electric Power Corporation (KEPCO) regional office locations

Korea Electric Power Corporation (KEPCO) divides the country into regional offices for operations. These divisions naturally reflect:

  • Service territory boundaries
  • Load density patterns
  • Maintenance and operations zones
  • Transmission network structure

KEPCO regional offices:

  • Approximately 200 regional offices nationwide
  • Each office serves a defined geographic area
  • Offices located at key substations
  • Distribution follows population and load density

Clustering procedure:

  1. Identify KEPCO office locations from public organizational data
  2. Assign each municipality to its nearest KEPCO office
  3. Aggregate demand and renewable capacity to office locations
  4. Validate clustering against population distribution

This approach naturally creates denser clustering in high-load areas (Seoul) and sparser clustering in rural regions.

  • Distance calculated using geographic coordinates (haversine formula)
  • Multiple municipalities may aggregate to one office

Data Processing and Allocation

Demand profile allocation:

  1. Municipal hourly profiles (230 × 8760 hours) from KPX data
  2. Aggregate to clusters based on KEPCO office assignment
  3. Preserve temporal patterns (hourly, daily, seasonal variations)
  4. Scale to match annual energy consumption statistics

Validation:

  • Peak demand matches reported system peak (~90 GW)
  • Seasonal patterns consistent with Korean grid (summer peak)
  • Daily profiles show realistic morning and evening peaks

Weather data allocation:

  1. LDAPS grid points (~5 km resolution) across Korea
  2. Map to bus locations (nearest grid point or spatial interpolation)
  3. Extract hourly time series for all 8760 hours
  4. Variables: Temperature, wind speed, radiation

Each of 193 buses assigned weather data from nearest LDAPS grid point, creating bus-specific microclimates.

Renewable generation profile generation:

Solar PV model:

  1. Solar irradiance from LDAPS radiation data
  2. PV conversion efficiency (typical ~15-20%)
  3. Temperature derating (panel efficiency vs. temperature)
  4. Tilt and orientation assumptions (south-facing, optimal tilt)

Wind power model:

  1. Wind speed at hub height (93m) from LDAPS
  2. Power curve model (cubic relationship in linear region)
  3. Cut-in speed: 3 m/s
  4. Rated speed: 12 m/s
  5. Cut-out speed: 25 m/s

Hydro model:

  1. Run-of-river assumption (limited storage)
  2. Seasonal streamflow patterns
  3. Weak correlation with recent precipitation
  4. Base capacity factor (~40-50% annual average)

Reference: Similar methodology to Western Wind and Solar Integration Study (NREL, 2013)

Generator allocation to buses:

  • Located at coastal areas (cooling water requirement)
  • Major plants: Southeastern coast (Busan area), western coast (Taean area)
  • Assigned to nearest bus in those regions
  • Capacity matched to reported unit sizes

LNG generators:

  • More geographically distributed
  • Located near major load centers
  • Assigned based on reported plant locations
  • Flexible generation for load following

Assignment validation:

  • Total capacity matches KEPCO statistics
  • Geographic distribution realistic
  • Generation mix: ~37% Coal, ~32% LNG, ~31% Nuclear

System Characteristics Preserved

1. Concentrated urban load:

  • Buses 7-18 (capital region area 1)
  • ~40% of total system load
  • High bus density (detailed representation)
  • Strong transmission network

Validation: Load distribution matches KEPCO regional statistics

2. Coastal generation concentration:

  • Buses around Busan area (Area 3)
  • Multiple nuclear units (> 8 GW)
  • Major coal plants co-located

Western Coal Belt:

  • Buses along western coast
  • Large coal plants (multiple GW)
  • Export power to capital region

Characteristic: Power flows long distances from coast to load centers, matching actual Korean grid pattern

3. Isolated grid structure:

  • Buses 187-193 (island)
  • Isolated from mainland except HVDC
  • 1500 MVA HVDC link (Buses 183-190)
  • Local generation and renewables

Represents Korea’s limited interconnection (no international connections).

4. Low renewable penetration:

  • ~10% installed capacity from renewables
  • Concentrated in southwestern regions (best solar resource)
  • Growing but still low compared to Europe/US
  • Realistic starting point for decarbonization studies

Validation

The clustered system validated through multiple methods:

Network feasibility:

  • AC power flow solved successfully for all hours
  • Voltage magnitudes within limits (0.95-1.1 p.u.)
  • No transmission overloads in base case

Solver: Ipopt (Interior Point Optimizer)

Unit commitment feasibility:

  • All days have feasible UC solutions
  • Sufficient capacity to meet demand + reserves
  • Commitment patterns realistic (baseload nuclear, flexible LNG)

Solver: HiGHS with 0.5% MIP gap

Demand-generation balance:

  • Total generation matches total demand + losses
  • Adequate capacity margin (~20-30%)
  • Peak load covered by available online capacity

Transmission adequacy:

  • Long-distance flows from coast to capital (realistic)
  • HVDC enables Jeju Island supply/export
  • No chronic bottlenecks in base topology

Limitations and Caveats

1. Simplified network topology:

  • Reactive power compensation devices (SVC, STATCOM)
  • Tap-changing transformers
  • Detailed substation configurations
  • Lower voltage (< 154 kV) distribution

Impact: AC OPF may not perfectly replicate voltage control in actual grid

2. OSM data uncertainty:

  • OpenStreetMap update schedule uncertain
  • Some connections may be outdated or incorrect
  • Line parameters estimated, not measured

Mitigation: Validated against known transmission structure, corrected obvious errors

3. Reactive power assumptions:

  • Q_d = 0.1 × P_d assumption
  • Actual reactive power varies more dynamically
  • No load-dependent reactive power model

Impact: May not capture reactive power challenges in actual grid

4. Aggregation effects:

  • 230 municipalities → 193 buses loses some detail
  • Load diversity within clusters averaged out
  • Some local network constraints not represented

Trade-off: Necessary for computational tractability in research studies

5. Temporal scope:

  • Weather patterns specific to 2022
  • No multi-year variability
  • Cannot study drought/flood impacts on long-term hydro

Extension: Can use different weather years with same network
